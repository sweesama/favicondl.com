How to Extract Favicons from Web Apps: A Dev's Guide (2026)

Ever tried to save a favorite website to your home screen, only to see a generic browser icon instead of the slick app icon you expected? Or maybe you're building a Progressive Web App (PWA) and need to verify all your app icons are correctly linked and available for extraction.

The thing is, when we talk about a web app's identity, a single favicon.ico isn't enough anymore. Modern web apps, especially PWAs, rely on a whole suite of icons defined across HTML <link> tags, Apple Touch Icon specifications, and crucially, the manifest.json file. Manually digging through all these definitions to get every single icon can feel like a scavenger hunt.

This is precisely where Mzu favicondl shines. It's not just for downloading a single favicon from a regular website (though it does that too, of course). It's built to help you comprehensively extract favicons from apps, meaning it parses all the places an app's icons might hide, giving you a complete picture.

What Mzu favicondl Does for Web App Icons

Think of Mzu favicondl as your digital archaeologist for app icons. Instead of just scanning for the most obvious <link rel="icon">, it dives deeper. It looks for all the different ways a web app declares its visual identity.

Specifically, it parses your HTML for traditional favicons and Apple Touch Icons (those crucial icons for iOS home screens). Then, it goes a step further and reads your manifest.json, pulling out every icon defined within its icons array. This means you get everything from the tiniest 48x48px icon to the largest 512x512px icon, including any specific purpose attributes like maskable.

The result? A single, consolidated view of every icon associated with that web app, ready for you to inspect and download. No more guessing which icon is used where, or missing a critical size for a specific device.

Getting Started: Extracting Your App's Icons

Using Mzu favicondl to extract your web app's icons is straightforward. Seriously, it's designed for speed, because who has time for fiddly manual processes?

  1. Navigate to Mzu favicondl: Open your browser and head over to Mzu favicondl.
  2. Enter the Web App URL: In the input field, paste the full URL of the web app you want to analyze (e.g., https://photos.google.com/).
  3. Hit Enter: Mzu favicondl will do its magic, scanning the page, its linked manifest, and any other icon declarations.
  4. Review and Download: You'll instantly see a comprehensive list of all detected icons, complete with their sizes, formats, and sources. From there, you can download individual icons or grab them all in a convenient zip file.
