How to Check Website Favicon: The Dev's Audit Guide

You’ve just deployed your site, and the icon looks perfect in your local Chrome tab. But then you check it on an iPhone, and it’s a generic gray square. Or worse, Google Search shows a broken image link next to your metadata. Checking a favicon isn't just about looking at your current browser; it’s about verifying how the rest of the world sees your brand.

How to Check Website Favicon Implementation

The most reliable way to perform a how to check website favicon audit is to use an external validator rather than your own browser. Browsers are notorious for aggressive favicon caching. You might update your favicon.ico, but your browser will keep showing the old one for weeks unless you force-clear the cache (which is a pain).

To get started, head over to Mzu favicondl. Paste your URL into the checker. The tool doesn't just look for a file; it parses your HTML head to find the specific declarations for different platforms. It checks for the standard ICO, the high-res PNGs, and the critical SVG manifests.

Key Features to Look For

When auditing your site, look for these three markers of a professional setup:

  • Path Validation: Are you using absolute paths? Relative paths can break on subdirectories.
  • Format Support: Does the site provide an apple-touch-icon? Without it, iOS users get a messy screenshot of your homepage instead of a clean logo.
  • Manifest Sync: If you use a site.webmanifest, the icons listed there must match your actual file structure.

Pro Tips for a Flawless Audit

Take a look at GitHub. They don't just have one icon; they serve different versions based on the environment and even the user's notification status. If you find your icons aren't appearing correctly, you should read our guide on favicon not showing to troubleshoot the common pathing errors.

My professional opinion? Stop relying on the /favicon.ico root fallback. While it works for legacy browsers, modern SEO and mobile UX demand explicit declarations. Always verify your Apple Touch Icon separately, as it’s the most common point of failure in a site audit. Using a tool like Mzu favicondl ensures you aren't blinded by your own browser's cache.
